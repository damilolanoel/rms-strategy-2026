Ekiti State — Political, Agricultural & Institutional Landscape
Everything you need to know about the operating environment before walking into the Ministry
The state of Ekiti agriculture right now

Ekiti State is a small landlocked state (5,887 sq km) in southwestern Nigeria with an estimated population of 3.5 million. Agriculture accounts for the largest share of livelihoods — between 65 and 70% of rural households are directly engaged in farming. The four dominant crops are yam (Nigeria's largest yam-producing state), cassava, cocoa (a major cash crop), and rice (a growing priority under the national food security agenda).

The state's agricultural performance is constrained by the same three structural problems seen across southwest Nigeria: inadequate input access (certified seed and fertiliser reach less than 30% of farmers), poor market infrastructure (most farmers sell at farm gate to itinerant traders, capturing 35–40% of retail price), and underperforming extension services (extension worker-to-farmer ratios far below the 1:750 FAO standard). Climate variability — shifting rainfall, longer dry seasons in the north, flooding in valley communities — is adding a fourth layer of stress.

The Oyebanji administration (elected 2022, re-elected 2026) has made agriculture a centrepiece of its six-pillar development agenda, specifically human capital development and economic development. Budget 2025 was ₦475.7 billion, with agriculture receiving capital allocations, though actual disbursement has been constrained by revenue shortfalls. The Ministry of Agriculture is one of the more active ministries in seeking donor partnerships and has existing relationships with World Bank FADAMA III+, APPEALS, and IFAD VCDP programmes.

Why the Ministry needs this research — right now

Budget defence pressure. The Ekiti State House of Assembly's Speaker has publicly demanded that all MDAs defend their budget proposals with evidence. The Ministry of Agriculture cannot defend capital programme allocations — farm settlement expansion, input subsidy programmes, market infrastructure investment — without data on current farmer status, programme outcomes, and need. Studies 1, 2, and 5 directly address this pressure.

Donor programme requirements. Every active donor programme in Ekiti State's agriculture sector requires independent baseline, monitoring, or evaluation data. World Bank FADAMA requires beneficiary surveys. IFAD VCDP requires market chain assessments. The EU Green Climate Fund requires climate vulnerability baselines. RMS's five studies are precisely the data products that unlock continued and expanded donor funding. The Commissioner knows this.

EKDIPA investor attraction. The Ekiti Development and Investment Promotion Agency (EKDIPA) actively pitches Ekiti to agricultural investors — but its investor pitch pack lacks credible, current production data. Study 1 (Agricultural Household Survey) generates exactly the data EKDIPA needs: yield per hectare by crop, input use rates, market gap analysis. This is a compelling cross-Ministry justification that the CEO should lead with.

Farm Settlement political urgency. The Farm Settlement Programme has been running for multiple cohorts but has never been independently evaluated. With a new administration having re-inherited the programme, there is political appetite for an honest assessment — both to claim credit for what works and to justify any redesign. Study 5 lands in a genuine political window.

Climate finance access. Nigeria's participation in the Global Climate Fund and Green Climate Fund requires state-level climate vulnerability assessments. Ekiti's environmental agencies are not equipped to produce this data. Study 4 provides the climate adaptation baseline and the climate finance readiness brief that the Ministry and EPA need to access international climate finance.

The procurement process — how government research gets commissioned in Ekiti State

How decisions are made. In Ekiti State, research and consultancy procurement follows the Ekiti State Public Procurement Law. Awards above a threshold (typically ₦10 million) require a procurement process — but Commissioner-directed sole-source procurement is common for specialised research where there is no competitive bid pool. RMS should position for direct award through the Commissioner's office, not through open tender where possible.

Budget timing. Ekiti State runs a January–December fiscal year. Budget is passed typically in Q1. Capital release for research and consultancy happens in Q2–Q3. The best time to get a research brief signed is April–June, when ministries have received budget releases and are looking to spend before the Q3 review. Present now, sign Q2.

The pathway. The typical pathway for an unsolicited proposal in Ekiti is: Director of Planning or Director of Statistics (technical entry point) → Permanent Secretary (administrative approval) → Commissioner (political sign-off) → Ministry of Finance (payment authorisation). The CEO should target the Commissioner and PS simultaneously — not work up from the technical level, which is slow.

Donor co-funding angle. If a donor programme (World Bank, IFAD, UNICEF) is willing to co-fund or directly commission the research, the procurement process is bypassed and procurement follows the donor's own rules. This is the fastest route for Studies 3, 4, and 5. The CEO should contact World Bank FADAMA III+ programme officers in Ado-Ekiti and IFAD VCDP coordinators directly, positioning RMS as the implementing research partner.

Study 01 · RMS-EK-GOV-2026-001
Agricultural Household Survey
Yam · Rice · Cocoa · Cassava
Hot priority n = 2,400 · 16 LGAs 14 weeks CAPI · GPS · Ekiti Yoruba
01
Why this study is the most important of the five

This is the foundation study. Every other study in this programme references a target population ("Ekiti farmers") — and without a current, representative household survey, none of the other studies can be properly framed, sampled, or weighted. Study 1 is the master sampling frame and baseline for the entire evidence programme.

More critically, Ekiti State has not conducted a comprehensive agricultural household survey in over a decade. The Ministry currently relies on FAO/CBN estimates and NBS AgriStat — both of which are national-level interpolations, not Ekiti-specific primary data. The Commissioner cannot tell you with confidence what yam yield per hectare is in Ikole LGA versus Ado. This study changes that permanently.

For EKDIPA, this is the single most valuable data asset the Ministry could produce. International agribusiness investors — Olam, ADM, Louis Dreyfus, Tolaro Global — need LGA-level production data, supply chain depth, and input use profiles before committing capital. Study 1 generates exactly that. The CEO should frame this as "Ekiti's investor prospectus for agriculture."

What the 9 survey modules actually reveal

The 9-module questionnaire is structured to generate 5 categories of output: (1) production baselines by crop and LGA, (2) input use and supply chain gaps, (3) post-harvest loss assessment, (4) market channel and price analysis, and (5) household income and welfare profiling. The GIS outputs — LGA-level choropleth maps of yield, input access, and market distance — are the visuals that will appear in EKDIPA investor pitches, Ministry press releases, and World Bank programme reports.

What this data unlocks

Budget defence: The Ministry can tell the House of Assembly exactly how many farmers are in each LGA, what they produce, and where investment gaps are — backed by n=2,400 household interviews, not estimates.

Input subsidy targeting: With data on which LGAs have lowest certified input use rates, the Ministry can target its subsidy budget precisely rather than distributing equally (which typically means urban-biased distribution).

FADAMA programme design: World Bank FADAMA requires beneficiary targeting data. This survey provides the population frame from which FADAMA beneficiary lists can be drawn.

Private sector partnerships: Agro-dealers, aggregators, and processors need volume and location data before establishing Ekiti operations. Study 1 provides this commercially.

Key risks to manage

Seasonal timing risk: Yam harvesting season in Ekiti runs October–December. Planting season is April–June. The questionnaire captures recall data, but accuracy is highest during or immediately after harvest. Confirm fieldwork timing with the Director of Agriculture.

Respondent trust: Farmers in Ekiti may be suspicious of data collection if they associate it with tax assessment or subsidy exclusion. The community liaison officer role and Ministry letter of authority are critical for response quality.

Plot size self-reporting bias: Farmers consistently under-report or over-report plot size. Build in GPS plot measurement for a 10% sub-sample to anchor self-reported data.

Study 02 · RMS-EK-GOV-2026-002
Farmer Access to Inputs, Credit & Markets
Hot priority n=1,600 + 8 FGDs + 16 IDIs 10 weeks Mixed methods
02
The structural problem this study investigates

Nigeria loses an estimated 40% of food crop value between farm and consumer due to supply chain inefficiencies. In Ekiti, this problem manifests as three compounding failures: input markets that are thin (few certified agro-dealers outside Ado-Ekiti), expensive (transport cost inflates urban input prices by 30–60% by the time they reach rural farmers), and unreliable (counterfeit fertiliser and seed are estimated to account for 30–40% of rural input sales nationally).

Credit is the other critical gap. Ekiti's formal agricultural credit penetration is estimated below 15%. The Bank of Agriculture (BOA) presence is limited to the state capital. Most smallholders finance their farming from personal savings, family transfers, or informal moneylenders at rates of 10–30% per month. The absence of formal credit is not just a welfare issue — it is the single largest constraint on input adoption and yield improvement.

On the market side, most Ekiti farmers sell at farm gate to itinerant traders (locally called "middlemen" or "onibara"), capturing 35–45% of final consumer price. The absence of organised market information means farmers cannot compare prices or hold produce for better offers.

The supply-side IDIs — what makes this study distinctive

Most agricultural access studies interview only farmers. This study interviews the supply side too: 6 agro-dealer managers (what their margin structure is, why they won't expand to rural areas), 4 microfinance bank credit officers (their actual agricultural loan default experience and the collateral requirements they impose), and 4 market traders (how price-setting works, where margins go). This triangulated picture is something the Ministry has never had.

What this data directly enables

Input subsidy programme redesign: If data shows that 65% of rural farmers cannot reach a certified agro-dealer within 10km, the Ministry knows exactly where to license new agro-dealer points — rather than subsidising inputs that farmers cannot access.

MFB engagement strategy: If data shows MFBs won't lend to farmers primarily due to collateral requirements (not credit risk), the Ministry can negotiate collateral-free agricultural credit schemes with specific banks — something several state governments have done successfully.

Market infrastructure investment prioritisation: LGA-level market access scores tell the Ministry exactly which LGAs need aggregation hub investment — a direct capital budget input.

IFAD VCDP alignment: IFAD's Value Chain Development Programme requires demand-side market assessments. This study's structure aligns with IFAD RIMS Level 2 indicators and can feed directly into VCDP supervision reports.

Methodological note — FGDs

The 8 FGDs are separated by gender (4 male groups, 4 female groups) specifically because female farmers in Ekiti face different market access barriers — restricted mobility, social norms around trading, spousal control of income. Mixing genders in FGDs suppresses female voice on these topics. The gender-separated design is a methodological commitment RMS should explicitly highlight in the Ministry pitch.

Study 03 · RMS-EK-GOV-2026-003
Agricultural Extension Service Effectiveness Evaluation
Politically sensitive n=1,200 + ~100 workers + 20 IDIs 11 weeks OECD-DAC framework
03
Why this study is politically sensitive — and how to handle it

This study evaluates the performance of the Ministry's own staff — the extension workers. That makes it the most politically sensitive of the five. Extension workers are civil servants with union representation. A study that shows low visit frequency or poor advisory quality will embarrass the Ministry's agricultural extension directorate and could be seen as threatening extension worker employment.

The CEO must frame this study very carefully in the Ministry pitch: this is not a performance audit of individual workers — it is a systems evaluation that examines why the extension system underperforms, with the aim of fixing it. The focus is on structural constraints (mobility, caseload, tools, supervision) not individual worker blame. Make this explicit in the pitch and in the proposal cover letter.

The political window for this study is actually good right now. The current administration inherited the extension system from its predecessor. Any weaknesses found are attributable to the old system. A new administration commissioning an honest evaluation signals governance confidence and gives the Commissioner political cover to implement reforms — which in turn strengthens their position with donors and the Governor.

The LGA Extension Effectiveness Index

The most powerful deliverable from this study is the LGA Extension Effectiveness Index — a ranked scorecard of all 16 LGAs on five dimensions: reach, advisory quality, adoption rates, efficiency, and sustainability. This index can be published (or selectively shared) to create internal competition among LGA Agriculture Officers to improve their scores. It becomes a management tool for the Director of Agricultural Extension.

What the Ministry can do with these findings

Redeployment decisions: Data on LGA-level extension worker-to-farmer ratios tells the PS exactly where redeployment would have the highest impact — without needing to commission a separate staffing review.

Training budget justification: If the evaluation shows that extension worker advisory quality is constrained by outdated training content (a common finding nationally), the Ministry can use this as evidence for an extension worker retraining budget line.

Digital extension tools case: If low visit frequency is driven by transport constraints (also a common finding), the evaluation creates the evidence base for a digital extension tool pilot — SMS advisory services, WhatsApp extension groups — which several donor programmes are willing to co-fund.

World Bank M&E: FADAMA III+ requires extension service performance data as part of its results framework. This evaluation satisfies that requirement and demonstrates the Ministry's commitment to evidence-based management to World Bank task teams.

Observation component — what it adds

The structured observation of 10 extension worker farm visits is unusual and adds significant credibility. It allows RMS to triangulate farmer self-report ("the extension worker visited once and said nothing useful") against what actually happens during visits. This direct observation component is what separates this from a standard satisfaction survey — and it's what the World Bank will note in the methodology review.

Study 04 · RMS-EK-GOV-2026-004
Climate Adaptation Practices Study Among Rural Farmers
Climate finance gateway n=1,600 · 3 agroeco zones 12 weeks GEF / GCF aligned
04
The climate finance opportunity — and why this study is the key

This study is the least obviously urgent to the Ministry — but it has the largest potential financial return. Nigeria is a signatory to the Paris Agreement and has National Adaptation Plans (NAPs) in place. Ekiti State, however, has no state-level climate vulnerability baseline, no documented adaptation practice inventory, and no community group capacity assessment. Without these, the state cannot access funds from the Green Climate Fund (GCF), the Global Environment Facility (GEF), the EU's GCCA+, or the African Development Bank's Climate Action Facility.

The GCF has allocated over $2 billion for African climate adaptation projects. Nigeria's access has been constrained partly by the absence of state-level evidence packages. Study 4 produces exactly the evidence package that the Federal Ministry of Environment and UNFCCC focal point need to submit an Ekiti State-level GCF funding proposal. This is a strategic opportunity worth multiples of the research cost.

The CEO should frame Study 4 as: "This study costs ₦[X]m and could unlock ₦500m+ in climate finance for Ekiti agriculture." No Commissioner can ignore that framing.

The three agroecological zones — why this matters

Ekiti is unusual in spanning three distinct zones: lowland forest (south), forest-savanna transition (centre), and derived savanna (north). Climate impacts differ materially across these zones — flooding risk is highest in the forest zone; drought stress is highest in the derived savanna; disease pressure from climate-sensitive pests varies by zone. A single state-level analysis would miss these differences. The zone-stratified design is technically essential — and it shows donors and climate scientists that RMS understands the agroecology of Ekiti.

The Climate Finance Readiness Package — what it contains

LGA Climate Vulnerability Index: Each of Ekiti's 16 LGAs scored on six dimensions: rainfall variability exposure, temperature stress, flood risk, drought frequency, crop yield sensitivity, and community adaptive capacity. This index can be reproduced in GCF proposals as the evidence of programme targeting rationale.

Community group capacity assessment: GCF and GEF require demonstration that community-level implementing groups exist and have the institutional capacity to manage programme funds. The study includes a community group capacity assessment across the 16 LGAs — covering governance structure, financial management, and gender inclusion.

Climate Finance Readiness Brief: A 2-page brief written specifically for World Bank, GEF, and GCF programme officers. Formatted to their standards, citing their indicators, demonstrating Ekiti's eligibility. The Commissioner can hand this brief directly to a visiting donor delegation.

NIMET data integration

The study integrates secondary agro-meteorological data from NIMET (Nigerian Meteorological Agency) and the Federal University of Technology Akure's climate records to contextualise farmer-reported observations against measured climate trends. This secondary data integration — which no local research firm routinely does — is what makes the climate vulnerability analysis scientifically credible to international reviewers.

Study 05 · RMS-EK-GOV-2026-005
Ekiti Farm Settlement Programme — Impact Assessment
Political window open Census + n=400 comparison 13 weeks PSM counterfactual
05
The political context for this evaluation

The Ekiti Farm Settlement Programme is a recurring feature of Ekiti State government — it has existed in various forms under multiple administrations. Each administration inherits it, modifies it somewhat, and continues it. The current administration is in its second term. The Farm Settlement Directorate wants to expand the programme. The Ministry of Finance wants to know if existing cohorts justify continued budget.

This is the ideal moment for an independent evaluation. The new administration can claim any positive findings as validation of their governance. Any problems found are attributable to programme design inherited from predecessors. And the Redesign Options Brief gives the current Commissioner three credible paths forward — all of which position them as evidence-driven, modern policymakers.

The CEO should note in the pitch: "An honest evaluation by an independent firm is the single most powerful tool you have to access World Bank and IFAD co-financing for the next phase of the programme. Without it, donors will not co-fund." This is true — IFAD's VCDP and the World Bank's FADAMA both require M&E evidence of past programme effectiveness before co-funding new phases.

The PSM counterfactual — why it matters

The most common weakness in government programme evaluations is the absence of a comparison group. Without one, you cannot tell whether observed improvements are due to the programme or to other factors (general economic growth, better rainfall, national input subsidies). Propensity Score Matching (PSM) constructs a statistically matched group of non-beneficiary farmers — matched on age, gender, LGA, crop, and farm size — and compares their outcomes to enrolled settlers. This is what international donors require for programme impact claims. No Ekiti-based consultancy currently offers this methodology.

Three unique deliverables that justify the premium

Programme Redesign Options Brief: Three fully costed redesign scenarios — e.g., (1) continue at current scale with targeted improvements, (2) restructure as a matching grant programme, (3) pivot to a commercial agribusiness incubator model. The Commissioner can present these to the Governor as evidence-based governance options, not just opinion.

Funder-Ready Impact Summary: A 4-page document formatted for World Bank, IFAD, and AfDB programme officers. This is the document that the Commissioner hands to a visiting World Bank task team. It contains: programme theory of change, counterfactual impact estimates, cost-effectiveness ratio, and a forward-looking investment case. This single document could unlock co-funding worth multiples of the evaluation cost.

PSM Technical Annex: The full statistical methodology — matching variables, balance diagnostics, sensitivity analysis, treatment effect estimates. For any external review or peer assessment, this annex demonstrates that the impact claims are methodologically defensible.

Managing the Farm Settlement Directorate

The Farm Settlement Directorate will be nervous. Their budget depends on the programme continuing. The CEO should meet the Director privately before the evaluation begins and explain: "Our job is to surface evidence, not to recommend closure. If the programme has problems, we will identify fixable root causes — not issue an execution order." Build them into the process — their cooperation on site access and beneficiary lists is essential.

How will you ensure data quality in rural Ekiti communities where enumerators may fabricate interviews?
This is the most important technical question you can ask, and the answer is our 8-layer QA system. Every interview is GPS-tagged — we can show you the exact coordinates of every interview on a map. 15% of interviews are audio-recorded. 10% are back-checked by phone within 48 hours. Our Field Manager monitors a daily paradata dashboard showing every enumerator's submission times, GPS locations, and average interview durations. An enumerator sitting at home fabricating interviews takes 3 minutes per questionnaire — but a genuine farmer interview takes 50 minutes. The timing data alone would expose fabrication. We've caught and dismissed enumerators for this in previous projects and will do so here.
🔴 Study 3 (extension evaluation) will demoralise our extension workers.
We have designed this evaluation specifically to avoid that outcome. Extension worker data is collected anonymously and reported only at LGA level — no individual worker is identified in any output. The evaluation framework focuses on systemic constraints (mobility, tools, caseload, supervision) rather than individual performance. In our experience, extension workers are often the most frustrated by the system's constraints — this evaluation gives them a formal channel to document those constraints and have them addressed. We would recommend a pre-evaluation briefing session with extension workers, facilitated by RMS, explaining the study purpose and protections. Workers who understand the evaluation typically become its strongest advocates.
Five studies is too ambitious — we don't have the internal capacity to manage five concurrent research projects.
You would not be managing five concurrent projects. You would be managing one relationship — with RMS. We handle all five studies under a single point of contact: one Project Director, one monthly progress call with the Ministry, one reporting format, one invoice schedule. The five studies are designed to share field infrastructure wherever possible: the same enumerator pool, the same community liaison network, the same QA system. The Ministry's interaction is a monthly oversight call and deliverable review — we handle all operational complexity. The Package Option is designed specifically to make this easy for you, not harder.
🔴 Our procurement rules require competitive bidding — we cannot award this directly to you.
We understand the procurement requirements of the Ekiti State Public Procurement Law. There are two clean options. First, under the Specialised Consultancy Services provision, the Commissioner has authority to issue a sole-source award for studies requiring specialised technical expertise — and we can provide the supporting justification document. Second, if you prefer a competitive process, RMS is willing to participate in a Request for Proposals — and we believe our methodology, team, and package pricing will be competitive. We are confident in an open process. Which route would the Ministry prefer?
What happens to our data after the study? Who owns it?
All data belongs entirely to the Ekiti State Ministry of Agriculture upon full payment. This is stated explicitly in Section 14 of every proposal and in our standard SOW. RMS receives no rights to publish, share, or monetise the data. The clean dataset, codebook, and all interview recordings are delivered to the Ministry. We delete our copies according to the agreed retention schedule. The Power BI dashboard is transferred to Ministry administration. The only thing RMS retains is the right to reference the study type in our credentials — not any findings.
Can you guarantee findings that support our programme?
No — and you should not want us to. Research that has been adjusted to show what the client wants is not research — it is a document of unknown credibility. When you present these findings to the World Bank, to the House of Assembly, or to the Governor, their value depends entirely on their independence. The moment it becomes known that findings were adjusted, every finding in every study loses its credibility — including the positive ones. We guarantee rigorous, honest methodology and findings. We guarantee you a fair hearing if you disagree with how we've presented something. But we cannot change findings. That is the value we provide.
Pitch Strategy — How to Win the Meeting
A step-by-step playbook for the first Ministry meeting, the clarification session, and the closing
Phase 1: Before the Meeting
Preparation checklist — 5 things to do before meeting the Commissioner

1. Read the Ekiti State 2025 budget agriculture chapter. The budget document is public. Find it, read the agriculture section, and identify specific programme names and allocations. Reference these by name in the meeting — it signals that you have done your homework and are not just a generic Lagos agency pitching a generic proposal.

2. Call the Director of Planning before the Commissioner meeting. Get a 15-minute phone call with the Director of Planning or Statistics first. Ask what data they currently rely on for planning decisions. Note the gaps they mention. Reference those specific gaps in the Commissioner meeting. The Director becomes your internal advocate if you've already engaged them technically.

3. Prepare one printed one-pager per study. Five A4 one-pagers — one per study — in a branded RMS folder. These stay on the Commissioner's desk after you leave. The pitch deck is on-screen; the one-pagers are what the PS reviews during budget meetings. Print on quality paper.

4. Know the current World Bank and IFAD programme names in Ekiti. Look up FADAMA III+ in Ekiti — know the programme coordinator's name if possible. Mention it by name: "We understand FADAMA III+ is active in Ekiti and our Study 1 data would integrate directly with its results framework." This signals donor awareness that most local consultancies lack.

5. Have pricing tiers ready but don't lead with price. Have three pricing scenarios prepared: (A) all five studies as a package, (B) Studies 1+2+5 as a priority cluster, (C) Study 1 alone as a pilot. Don't mention price until the Commissioner asks. Lead with value, end with investment.

Phase 2: The Meeting
Opening — first 3 minutes (say this almost verbatim)
"Commissioner, thank you for the time. We are not here to sell you research — we are here because Ekiti State has a specific evidence gap that is costing the Ministry money and limiting donor access. Let me be direct about what that gap is, what it enables, and what it would take to close it. Then I'd like to hear your priorities and see where the fit is."
The evidence gap — say this if the Commissioner seems sceptical
"Commissioner, I'll ask you one question. When the House of Assembly agriculture committee asks you next month to justify the Farm Settlement budget — what data do you cite? If the answer is 'our internal records and estimates,' that's the gap. These five studies give you independent, third-party validated evidence that no committee chairman can dismiss."
The donor angle — use this if budget is raised as an objection
"I understand budget is constrained. Here is an option we haven't mentioned yet: Studies 1, 2, and 4 are directly co-fundable through FADAMA, IFAD-VCDP, and the Green Climate Fund. RMS can draft the co-funding request letter as part of our engagement. In a best case, the Ministry's net investment could be 30–40% of the sticker price. Would it help if we mapped the donor co-funding options for each study before our next meeting?"
The package close — say this at the end
"We've structured these as five individual studies, but the real value is in the integrated evidence programme — one partner, shared field infrastructure, one dashboard, consistent methodology. The package pricing reflects that integration. I'd like to propose that we agree in principle on Studies 1 and 5 today, and I'll come back with a formal SOW and package price for all five within the week. Is that a reasonable next step?"
Phase 3: Closing the Deal
Week 0 — First meeting
Leave five one-pagers + pitch deck on USB
Do not leave printed proposals (too long to read at this stage). One-pagers only. Follow up within 48 hours with a short email and the five proposals as PDF attachments.
Week 1 — Technical follow-up
Meet the Director of Planning / Statistics
Separate from the Commissioner meeting. This is a 45-minute technical session — walk through Study 1 methodology in detail. Answer their technical questions. This person will brief the PS on whether RMS is credible. Win them.
Week 2 — Clarification session
Request formal clarification meeting + presentation
Use the 20-slide pitch deck. Present to Commissioner + PS + Director of Planning together. Address any outstanding questions. Ask directly: "Which studies are you most interested in commissioning first?" — this is a buying signal question.
Week 3 — Package proposal
Submit revised SOW with final pricing
Include three scenarios: All 5 (package rate), Priority 3 (Studies 1+2+5), Pilot (Study 1 only). Include the donor co-funding mapping. Make it easy to say yes to at least the pilot option.
Week 4–5 — Negotiation
Expect one round of pricing negotiation
Government clients almost always negotiate. Build a 10–15% negotiation buffer into your initial pricing. Be willing to reduce scope before reducing margin — offer to drop a deliverable rather than cut day rates.
Week 5–6 — Close
PO / LOA → SOW signing → Mobilisation
Push for DocuSign / electronic signing to accelerate. Once SOW is signed, mobilisation payment has a 7-day target. Have your field team on standby from Week 4 so you can launch within days of fund receipt.
What Ultimately Wins This — The Non-Negotiables
Seven things that determine whether RMS wins this programme or loses it to a competitor
1 · Personal access to the Commissioner — not just the Director

The single most important determinant of a government research contract in Nigeria is whether the CEO has a direct relationship with the Commissioner. Not an email — a meeting, a phone call, a WhatsApp message. The Commissioner is the only person who can direct the Ministry to commission research. If the CEO only meets the Director of Planning, the proposal will sit in a file for six months. Get to the Commissioner directly. Use every network connection you have — LCCI, professional associations, mutual contacts, government relations firms if necessary. One 20-minute meeting with the Commissioner is worth more than ten emails to the Director.

2 · Demonstrating you understand Ekiti specifically — not just "agriculture"

Nigerian government officials are experienced at identifying generic proposals that have been recycled from other states. Your proposals are Ekiti-specific (they reference the Farm Settlement Programme, EKDIPA, FADAMA in Ekiti, and the agroecological zones) — but you must reinforce this specificity verbally in the pitch. Know that Ekiti is Nigeria's largest yam producer. Know that the derived savanna in the north faces different climate challenges than the forest south. Know the names of the programmes the Ministry is running. This specificity signals genuine preparation and distinguishes RMS from every other agency that sends a generic capability statement.

3 · The donor co-funding angle — make it concrete, not theoretical

Saying "this study can be co-funded by donors" is common in proposals. What wins is making it specific and actionable: "We have already spoken with the World Bank FADAMA coordinator about Study 1 data integration, and they expressed strong interest in discussing co-funding." Even an exploratory call with a donor programme officer before the Ministry meeting transforms a theoretical claim into a concrete one. If the Commissioner thinks there's a real chance the Ministry only pays 40% of the cost, the urgency to commission immediately increases dramatically. Make those calls before the pitch.

4 · Offering the pilot option — lower risk, same relationship

Every government official is risk-averse about a first engagement with an unknown vendor. The pilot option — commission Study 1 alone, evaluate quality, then proceed to Studies 2–5 — removes the barrier to getting started. It also means you have a foot in the door: once Study 1 is underway and the Ministry can see the quality of your fieldwork and reporting, the remaining four studies become much easier to close because the risk has been demonstrated to be low. Resist the temptation to push for all five from day one if the Commissioner is hesitant. A signed Study 1 is worth infinitely more than a pending five-study proposal.

5 · Demonstrating data quality obsession — not just claiming it

Every consultancy tells a government client that their data is high quality. The way to make this claim credible is to be specific: "We GPS-tag every interview and we can show you the map of every interview location from our last project." "We audio-audit 15% of interviews and send the supervisor-reviewed files to the client." "Our back-check rate is 10% and we report back-check discrepancy rates in every weekly progress report." Specificity converts a generic claim into a verifiable commitment. Bring a printed example of a field QA daily report from a previous project (anonymised) to the technical meeting with the Director of Planning.

6 · The Power BI dashboard as a living decision tool

This is RMS's single strongest differentiator in the Ekiti pitch and it should be demonstrated — not just described. Before the first Ministry meeting, build a demo Power BI dashboard using publicly available Ekiti agriculture data (NBS AgriStat, KNBS, FAO Nigeria data). Show it on a laptop in the meeting. Let the Commissioner filter by LGA. Let them click on a crop and see the data update. This 5-minute demonstration does more for winning the contract than 20 slides of methodology. No other research agency pitching Ekiti agriculture is doing this. It is immediately differentiating.

7 · Speed — the government client's most underrated need

Government commissioners live in a constant cycle of pressure — budget defences, donor supervision missions, Assembly scrutiny, Governor's reviews. What they need most is a research partner who can turn around quality work faster than any other option. RMS's timelines (Study 1: 14 weeks, Study 2: 10 weeks) are already faster than university research centres or international consultancies would promise. In the pitch, emphasise: "We can have Study 1 fieldwork launched within 3 weeks of SOW signing. Your topline findings can be in your hands before the next Assembly budget review." Timing the delivery of findings to a known political or budget milestone is one of the most powerful sales tools in government research.
